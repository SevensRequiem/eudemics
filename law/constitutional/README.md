# Constitutional Law

Foundational constraints that override everything else.

## Foundation

The US Constitution is a strong starting point. Its core principles (individual rights, separation of powers, due process, protection from government overreach) align with the model.

What the model expands:
- Competency requirements for governance (the Constitution assumed voters would be informed)
- Node-based federalism replacing state/federal tension
- Explicit protections against corporate capture of governance
- Technology-aware rights (data sovereignty, AI rights, genetic autonomy)

## Constitutional Mandates

These cannot be overridden by statutory law or node decision:

- **Archiving and data redundancy** - the complete record of human knowledge and digital activity is a public good. Wayback Machine equivalents receive state funding or are state-absorbed. No entity can erase the archive. The archive is inviolable.
- **Historical preservation** - revisionism is unconstitutional. The record is append-only. No node majority can vote to rewrite history.
- **Basic rights are unconditional** - dignity, care, safety, existence. These do not vary with competency, contribution, or status.
- **Evidence-based policy** - legislation must be grounded in evidence. Ideology alone is not sufficient basis for law.
- **Stewardship principle** - the state guides, it does not force. This is constitutional, not just cultural.
